Why Reston Homes Have Unique Exterior Needs

Reston was designed in the 1960s with an intentional integration of housing and nature, and that design philosophy still defines the community today. Nearly every home sits in close proximity to mature trees — which means persistent shade on roof surfaces, high leaf debris loads in gutters, and significant moisture retention on north-facing exterior walls. These conditions accelerate the breakdown of siding, accelerate granule loss on shingles, and create ideal conditions for mold and algae growth on soffits and fascia.

Reston's cluster associations and the Reston Association itself have specific standards for exterior work — including approved material lists and color palettes for many neighborhoods. Our team is experienced in navigating these requirements and regularly provides homeowners with the documentation and samples needed to obtain HOA approval before scheduling work.

Reston's housing stock spans from original 1960s and 70s construction to homes built in the 1990s, meaning there is a wide range of original materials in play. Some homes still have original masonite or T1-11 siding that is badly deteriorated. Others have aluminum or early vinyl that is cracking and fading. We assess each home individually and specify the right replacement solution for its construction type.

Common Questions from Reston Homeowners

Does Reston Association need to approve my roofing or siding project?

Reston Association (RA) requires homeowners to submit a Design Review Board application for exterior changes, including roofing material or color changes and siding replacements. Many cluster associations have additional requirements layered on top of RA rules. We are experienced with this process and can assist you in preparing the required submission materials — specifications, color samples, and product data sheets — before work is scheduled.

My Reston townhome roof was last replaced in 2001 — is it time?

A 25-year-old roof is well past its typical service life, particularly in Northern Virginia's climate. Heavy shade from Reston's tree canopy accelerates granule loss and moisture retention, which can shorten a roof's effective lifespan below the manufacturer's rated warranty. We recommend a free inspection to assess current condition, granule wear, flashing integrity, and ventilation — all of which inform whether repair or full replacement makes more sense at this stage.

Can you match the original siding profile on my 1970s Reston home?

In most cases, yes. James Hardie's HardiePlank lap siding comes in a range of exposure widths and profiles that closely replicate the look of original wood lap and board-and-batten siding common on Reston's older homes. We specify the profile that best matches your home's original design during the estimate, and we provide a sample board for HOA review if required.

Are gutter guards worth it for Reston homes given the heavy tree cover?

Gutter protection systems can significantly reduce the frequency of required gutter cleaning in heavily wooded areas like Reston, but the quality of the product matters enormously. We offer micromesh gutter guard systems that block debris while allowing full water flow — and unlike cheap foam inserts or surface-tension covers, they hold up well under Reston's heavy maple seed and oak leaf load. We'll give you an honest assessment of whether they make sense for your specific property during your free estimate.

How do you handle tree branches near the roof during a roofing project?

We work carefully around existing vegetation and do not trim or remove trees as part of our scope. If branches are close enough to interfere with safe installation, we will flag that in advance and recommend you arrange for trimming before we begin — a qualified arborist is the right resource for that work. We protect landscaping and hardscaping during installation with drop cloths and staged material placement, and we do a thorough cleanup of all debris at project completion.
