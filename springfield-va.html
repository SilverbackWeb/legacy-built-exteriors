Common Questions from Springfield Homeowners

My Springfield home is from the 1960s. What should I expect from a roof inspection?

On a 60-year-old home, we look beyond just the shingles. We examine the decking for soft spots and rot, assess the ventilation system for adequacy, check all flashing points around chimneys and penetrations, and look for signs of previous water intrusion in the attic. Homes from this era often have had two or three shingle installations over the original deck, and we need to assess decking condition thoroughly before committing to a project scope and price.

Is James Hardie siding appropriate for a 1960s or 1970s Springfield home?

Absolutely. James Hardie fiber cement is an excellent choice for Springfield's older homes because it can be installed over properly prepared sheathing without requiring the structure to be modified. It replicates the look of original wood siding while eliminating the rot, pest, and paint maintenance problems that make wood challenging on aging homes. We'll assess your home's specific sheathing and substrate condition before recommending a full installation approach.

How do I know if my gutters need replacement or just cleaning?

Gutters that are pulling away from the fascia, sagging between hangers, leaking at seams, or showing visible rust and corrosion typically need replacement — cleaning won't fix structural problems. On Springfield's older homes, we often find gutters that have been patched multiple times and are simply past their service life. We'll assess your gutters as part of a free inspection and give you a straight answer on whether cleaning can extend their life or replacement makes more sense.

Does Fairfax County require permits for work on older homes?

Yes. Fairfax County requires permits for full roof replacements and most siding work regardless of the home's age. On older homes, the inspection process can also surface code compliance items related to ventilation and structural details that need to be addressed. We handle all permit applications and coordinate the inspection process — homeowners don't need to manage any of this.
